# Sunita Williams on 286 Days in Space, NASA Missions & Astronaut Mindset | FO461 Raj Shamani

## Summary
Indian-American NASA astronaut Sunita Williams sits with Raj Shamani after returning from her unexpected 286-day mission aboard the ISS — a 10-day Boeing Starliner test flight that turned into a 9-month stay after thruster failures. She has logged 608 days in space (2nd most for any NASA astronaut), 9 spacewalks, was the first person to run a marathon in space, and has commanded the ISS twice.

## Childhood & Identity
- Grew up in the only (partly) Indian family in her US town — father is Indian, mother Slovenian, multicultural and multi-religious.
- Was a competitive swimmer; came to school with wet hair and "always stuck out."
- Insecurities throughout her career: "Am I going to pass this check ride? Am I going to know how to do a spacewalk?"
- Believes everyone has insecurities — "if you don't, you're not being introspective."

## Path to NASA
- Wasn't a straight shot; helicopter pilot wasn't her first choice. She got exposed to space through friends who were astronauts.
- Advice for kids: stop asking "what do you want to be when you grow up" — ask "what are you interested in" and expose them to real adults doing different jobs.

## Astronaut Training
- Written/paper psych testing plus group problem-solving exercises (e.g., "move this table over a bridge") to surface natural leaders and team players.
- NOLS (National Outdoor Leadership School) — backpacking/rafting in uncomfortable settings to find your hot spots. Sunita learned she's a neat-freak who gets anxious around messy roommates.
- "You can't change other people."
- Plans for hundreds of failure scenarios; trains for 2 years per launch.

## The Starliner Drama
- After launch, **5 thrusters failed** during approach to the ISS. She and Butch Wilmore "looked at each other — this is not so good."
- Compartmentalized: solve docking first, worry about return later. ("Like *The Martian* — one bite at a time.")
- Mission Control bypassed flight rules, restarted engines, regained 6-axis control, docked successfully.
- That whole summer was the hardest part — uncertainty about return date, family expectations to manage.
- They knew by August they wouldn't come home until the next scheduled spacecraft (~6 months later).
- **Never lost hope** — knew thousands of people on the ground were working the problem. They had a Plan B: makeshift seats designed in the Dragon capsule before Starliner left.

## The Public Narrative vs. Reality
- The "stuck astronauts" framing was largely a media exaggeration. Family wasn't worried because she was talking to them constantly. NASA-Boeing miscommunications fed the press cycle.
- Lessons learned for future missions: a proper seat kit and Y-cable air connection so any astronaut can transfer between spacecraft if needed.

## Life in Space
- 16 sunrises and 16 sunsets per 24 hours; time perception varies by activity level.
- **Crying in space**: tears ball up — "you'll be like a fish in an aquarium." Still cries — missed her dogs and friends.
- **Bathroom**: no gravity-assist; have to commit. Lots of farting (preservatives, possibly).
- **Smell**: not as bad as expected; food smells linger — Indian food, garlic, fish/salmon.
- **Sounds**: ISS creaks and pops as the orbit moves it through sun/shade. Alarming on first mission, familiar by the third.
- **Light flashes** seen with eyes closed at night = cosmic radiation hitting the brain.
- **Closest brush with fear**: A satellite below them disintegrated, debris field forced everyone into "safe haven" spacecraft. The crew met one last time at the bathroom — "hopefully I'll see you on Earth."

## Returning to Earth
- Felt nauseous for 24–48 hours. Inner-ear/balance system "shut down" in zero-G needs to recalibrate.
- ~1 month to fully recover small balance muscles, neck muscles.
- First meal back: a grilled-cheese sandwich with husband's homemade bread — a tribute to her late father, who introduced her to grilled cheese (the first cheese he ever ate was on his ship from India).

## Her Father (Deepak Pandya, neuroscientist)
- "His accomplishments were so much more brave and courageous than mine."
- Came from India to the US with no parents to guide him; decided alone to attend boarding school, medical school, and emigrate.
- Gave Sunita the **Bhagavad Gita and Upanishads** for her second flight; she carried Lord Ganesha and the Gita in space again.
- Inherited from him: the ability to stop and observe the world. From her mother: "Slovenian stubbornness."
- He passed peacefully — she and her sister arrived just after; describes it as if he was simply ready to move on.

## On Death & Risk (Re: Kalpana Chawla)
- Kalpana, Dave Brown and the Columbia crew were friends. Their loss didn't scare her — it pushed her to keep exploring "in their memory."
- "We're in this business because it's risky. You accept some of those risks."
- Strapping into a rocket = "like the start of a football match. We're ready. Let's go."

## India From Space
- Daytime: hazy at times; the Himalayas are spectacular; river runoff into the ocean creates beautiful color swirls.
- Nighttime: cities lit up like "connected nerves" — a network of white lights.
- Took a photo of the **India-Sri Lanka link** that "caused a little discussion." (Likely Adam's Bridge / Ram Setu — she leaves it implied: "There's something there.")
- New phenomenon: massive lit-up fishing-boat clusters off India's west coast.

## On Aliens
- "Absolutely" believes life exists out there — billions of stars, microbes proven to live on the outside of the ISS in vacuum.
- Eyes Europa (Jupiter's moon) as a candidate. "Probably not humans, but some forms of life."
- Most beautiful sight: the aurora — in summer 2024 the ISS flew *through* it (greens, pinks, purples).

## Biggest Takeaway From This Mission
"People are good." Strangers from every nation, religion, and politics told her "welcome home, we were praying for you." The press narrative blew everything up; the actual humans underneath are kind. "We just need to listen to each other."

## Tone
Calm, grounded, generous — repeatedly downplays her own achievements while crediting team, family, and her father.
