# Selena Gomez: "I Didn't Recognize Myself Anymore… Then I Finally Let Go and Found Who I Really Am" | On Purpose with Jay Shetty

## Summary
Jay Shetty interviews Selena Gomez (and later her mother Mandy Teefey) around the release of Selena's documentary *My Mind & Me*. The conversation covers vulnerability, depression, bipolar diagnosis, fame's impact, philanthropy, and a deeply moving mother-daughter dynamic.

## Selena on the Documentary
- Wrestled internally about releasing it but ultimately did because "it wasn't just about me."
- Watching it back, she "didn't recognize that girl anymore" and felt heartbroken at how she once spoke about her body and image.
- Releasing it is itself a healing — letting go of an old version of herself.

## Living With Bipolar Disorder
- Has been to four treatment centers.
- Through DBT and CBT, learned to "recognize what's happening, accept it" rather than expecting it to be fixed.
- "I have bipolar — I just learned to live with it. I've made it my friend."
- Combines therapy + medication with practical adjustments (changing music, what she watches, scary movies on rotation).

## Key Themes
- **Imperfection as identity**: Halloween story of being a $5 banana costume in Times Square while everyone else went sexy and elaborate — "that sums up who I am."
- **Surrendering perfectionism in work**: Mistakes in the studio or on set become the best hooks/jokes; she craves those moments.
- **Boundaries on whom you confide in**: Sharing your darkest secrets requires the right people; bad advice can sink you deeper.
- **Mom's wisdom**: When Selena was scared of tornadoes, her mom got her books on weather — "if you're afraid of something, learn more about it."
- **"Blessing in the breaking"** (Jay's framing) — every encounter from road rage to grief is an opportunity to make a better choice.

## On Fame & Press
- Has had to take control of her own narrative; chooses to lead with love and inject love rather than be defensive.
- Frustrated by interviewers asking buzzy clickbait ("what word do you associate with marshmallow?") instead of meaningful questions.
- Doesn't want to be the celebrity who has it "all together" — wants to feel like a friend people can walk up to.

## Trip to Africa
Met a woman named Betty who, while battling suicidal thoughts, had sat alone by a lake for two weeks contemplating ending her life. Selena was floored. Realized mental health crosses every culture; the same loneliness/feeling-not-enough surfaces everywhere.

## Wondermind & Philanthropy
- Selena and Mandy co-founded *Wondermind* — a "mental fitness" platform targeting feelings (not just diagnoses), sharing daily content and accessible resources.
- The goal traces back to Selena's only suicide attempt at age 7. "Nothing bad was happening — I just felt like everything was wrong."
- Rare Beauty's Rare Impact Fund: pledged to raise **$100M for mental health** in underserved communities.

## Mandy's Story (the mother's segment)
- Got pregnant at 16. "Selena came at the perfect time — I had someone responsible to be."
- Hasn't watched the documentary fully — fears reliving moments she didn't know Selena was going through.
- Therapist's prescription during her own breakdown (after a miscarriage): leave the house once a day, buy a movie ticket, get receipts. After six weeks, the world stopped feeling judgmental.
- Realized her parenting mistake: "I was parenting her the way I needed to be parented, not what she was needing."

## On Therapy & Self-Knowledge
Selena: "I had to learn how to understand my mind on my own. Some doctors said 'you're hallucinating' when I described seeing a black butterfly — it was a real black butterfly."

## The "Final Five"
- Best mental-health advice: make it your friend; "take your time."
- Worst: "It's all in your head, just control it."
- Current purpose (Selena): be loved the way she loves; give the way people have given to her.
- Used to value: people's opinions.
- One world law: forgiveness; treat others as you wish to be treated.

## Tone
Emotional, sisterly, healing-oriented; Selena is articulate and self-aware about being still in process.
